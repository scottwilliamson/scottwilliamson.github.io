SOLITAIRE (SOLITAIRE English version)

« Solitaire » is a French word meaning “solitary," “alone" or “recluse." Foreseeing, peut-être, the oppressiveness of the imminent 24/7 culture, the original 1981 French version of the song “Solitaire" as written by Martine Clémenceau extols the virtues of being a hermit, perhaps in the mountains where there are no computers (!)

There are also, she suggests, no tenants, no lodgers, no commentary. You come out of the void, are closer to the gods and die like a giant. As you do at great altitudes.

Sung with verve and conviction by Clémenceau herself and closed with some jammin' horns, the song charted for months in France but, paradoxically, never went very high.

The 1983 English language version starts out with a slightly more subdued instrumentation but then swings for the fences. The song plays off the stronger connotation for Anglos of “Solitaire" as a card game played alone. The narrator describes her loneliness at not being needed, being almost abandoned by her lover and observes that “all the cards were held by you."

A song of empowerment, though, it turns the tables and gives her lover a change of heart, after she has gotten over him. She informs him that “you found out you loved me just a little too late"; it will now be him crying all night and playing Solitaire.

“Don't wait up 'cuz babe, I won't be there" concludes this Aesop's fable of a song, sung by Laura Branigan with passion rivaling Clémenceau's. It was a top ten hit in America.
